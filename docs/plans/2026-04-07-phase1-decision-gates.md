## Problem Statement

High school students don't know how to solve wicked problems. A linear task checklist teaches them the wrong mental model — that customer discovery is a sequence of tasks, not a process of evidence-based decision making.

## Demand Evidence

- 871 participants starting this week (April 7, 2026)

## Status Quo

Without intervention, teams will:
- Check off activities without understanding why
- Lock onto fake problems early
- Waste Phases 2-3 building solutions nobody needs
- Learn the wrong lesson about problem-solving (tasks = progress)

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Target:** The team that's completely lost in Phase 1 — doesn't know what "good" looks like, can't tell if their problem is real or fake.

**Wedge:** Add 3 decision checkpoints that force teams to stop, reflect, and decide: Kill / Pivot / Proceed.

## Premises

1. ✅ Linear checklist is wrong for wicked problems
2. ✅ Evidence quality > activity completion
3. ✅ Teams need decision points, not more content
4. ✅ Phase 1 quality determines entire hackathon outcome

## Approaches Considered

**Approach A: Decision-Gate Redesign**
Restructure 16 activities into 4 decision gates with go/no-go moments.
- Effort: M
- Risk: Low
- Pros: Ships fast, teaches right mental model
- Cons: Requires rewriting activity structure

**Approach B: Spiral Learning Model**
3 spirals (Learn → Try → Reflect → Decide), each getting tighter.
- Effort: L
- Risk: Medium
- Pros: Best pedagogy, visually distinct
- Cons: New UI, might not ship this week

**Approach C: Minimal Viable Redesign ✅**
Keep 16 activities, add 3 decision checkpoints after activities 5, 10, 14.
- Effort: S
- Risk: Low
- Pros: Ships fastest, still breaks linear illusion
- Cons: Less elegant than full redesign

## Recommended Approach

**Approach C** — Minimal Viable Redesign. Ships this week, uses existing activity engine, no new UI code required.

## Implementation Plan

### Checkpoint 1 (after activity 5: Create Persona)
**Title:** "Checkpoint 1: Real User or Just a Guess?"
**Instructions:** Stop. Look at your persona. Can you name an actual human who matches this? If not, pivot back to activities 2-4. If yes, proceed.
**Assessment:** Text answer — "Who is your real user? Name them. What specific evidence do you have that they exist?"
**Gate:** Mentor review (auto-pass if evidence is specific, flag if vague)

### Checkpoint 2 (after activity 10: Discuss in Group)
**Title:** "Checkpoint 2: Validated or Assumed?"
**Instructions:** You've interviewed and synthesized. Is this problem based on real evidence or assumptions? Be honest.
**Assessment:** Kill/Pivot/Proceed selector + text justification
**Gate:** Auto-pass with reflection logged for mentor visibility

### Checkpoint 3 (after activity 14: Submit 3 Trustful Sources)
**Title:** "Checkpoint 3: Ready for Mentor Review?"
**Instructions:** Final check before submission. Do you have enough evidence to defend this problem to a mentor?
**Assessment:** Confidence slider (0-100%) + "What's your weakest piece of evidence?"
**Gate:** Triggers mentor booking workflow

## Open Questions

- Should checkpoints be numbered activities (6, 11, 15) or labeled as "Checkpoint A/B/C"?
- Should mentors actually review Checkpoint 1, or just flag vague answers?
- What happens if a team selects "Kill" at Checkpoint 2? Do they loop back or exit?
